Understanding AI Safety: Full Letter

Home | Full Letter

Overview: Many policy initiatives aim to increase AI safety. Recently, SB-1047, a bill in California, proposes to impose liability on model developers for downstream use, to incentivize greater safety investment from model developers. While well-intentioned, this approach has sparked significant debate in the AI community, due to its potentially unintended consequences that hinder innovation and open-source AI efforts. 

AI is a complex and immature technology: increasing AI safety requires building a robust scientific foundation that is still fairly nascent today. In particular, if policymakers are to pursue highly committal policy, evidence of the risks of AI should meet a high evidentiary standard. Further, they should actively consider the potential costs of policy in terms of both the covered entities that must comply as well as society more broadly.

We argue that there are higher priorities we should explore that may offer more effective solutions. These alternatives address more immediate and direct needs for improving AI safety and have fewer negative consequences. Thus, instead of taking a liability-based approach to regulation (as taken in SB-1047), we believe it's prudent to first consider and implement the following suggested priorities:

  • We need to better understand AI risks.
  • We need to increase transparency on AI development.
  • We need to actively monitor post-deployment AI harms.
  • We need to develop and deploy mitigation and defense mechanisms for identified AI risks.
  • We need to build trust and reduce fragmentation in the AI community. 

We describe each of these priorities in more detail below. We believe by following these steps we can pave a more productive path toward robust and responsible AI development.

First, we need to better understand AI risks. As AI models rapidly advance in capability, our level of investment to understand risks should also increase. However, our understanding of how these models function and their possible negative impacts on society remains very limited. Because this technology has the potential to be far more powerful than pre-existing technologies and carries wide-reaching implications, it is crucial to have a comprehensive understanding of AI risks. Moreover, a comprehensive understanding of AI risks is the foundation and prerequisite for effective regulations and policies. Therefore, we recommend extensive study of different types of risks from AI, including AI risks from misuse, systemic risks from unintended consequences, and loss of control(e.g., models’ autonomous deception of developers). We recommend such studies to be conducted within a framework of marginal risks, assessing the additional societal risks posed by AI models compared to pre-existing technologies like internet search engines. This approach will allow for identifying AI's unique risks and its incremental negative impacts on society. In particular, we recommend extensive and comprehensive study of a broad spectrum of different misuse risks with a framework of marginal risks, including scams, misinformation, non-consensual intimate imagery, child sexual abuse material, cybersecurity risks, and biosecurity risks, which can significantly negatively impact society in the near future. Given our current limited understanding of risks and the lack of a framework to evaluate them, we recommend allocating resources to both academia and industry to foster research on AI risk analysis. For example, establishing a dedicated research institute or center can accelerate comprehensive studies on various AI risks and facilitate the creation of benchmarks and mechanisms to identify and analyze emerging risks. Ultimately, these risk analyses and resource investments will help inform decisions on important policy questions such as whether to develop or allow the use of a model, and whether it should be open or closed source.

Second, we need to establish processes for transparency. Analyzing model risks and informing policies is challenging without adequate model information. This often requires model transparency (as mandated by the Biden executive order on AI). Transparency requirements for advanced, closed models would facilitate risk analysis of capable models with potentially significant impacts. A number of important questions related to transparency require further study. One key question is how to determine effective criteria. Currently, while regulatory frameworks such as EO and EU AI Act often set the thresholds based on certain numbers for compute power or dollar amount, there is no solid evidence behind these numbers. More rigorous studies would be required to establish criteria that strike the right balance between innovation and risk mitigation. Furthermore, we need to study various transparency levels to balance usefulness, information disclosure, and potential overhead for model developers. Key questions include: what information should be shared with different parties (e.g. the public, trusted third parties, the government), and what information developers should report among various model information including model sizes, a summary of training data and methods, model capabilities (including test results on certain benchmarks and red teaming practices), and incidents such as model theft, unauthorized access, and inadvertent release of model weights. In line with this approach, the establishment of a state registry or a national registry for capable models can improve transparency. 

Third, we need to develop an early warning detection mechanism. While current model capabilities may not pose devastating consequences, given the rapid pace of technological development, significantly more powerful AI models are likely to emerge in the near future. It's crucial to establish mechanisms to detect early warning signs as soon as possible, providing society time to implement stronger measures, and preventing devastating consequences and crossing predefined red lines. The mechanism should encompass both in-lab testing and real-world monitoring. In a lab setting, we need to detect and evaluate the dangerous capabilities of models through rigorous evaluation and red teaming. This involves testing AI models with adversarial scenarios to uncover potential vulnerabilities or unintended behaviors, and assessing how the models could lead to significant marginal risks in areas such as cybersecurity and biochemical weapons. Such evaluation and red teaming analyses can help identify risks before models are deployed in the real world. After the models are launched, the mechanism should enable adverse event reporting, documenting how AI has been used for misuse and what consequences it has caused in the real world. To this end,continuous real-world monitoring of how misuse of AI may have caused harm in each application domain such as biotechnology and cybersecurity is crucial. Moreover, we need to determine to whom these early warnings should be reported and design a responsible report protocol. This will help ensure that identified risks are communicated effectively among the relevant authorities or stakeholders.

Fourth, we need to develop technical mitigation and defense mechanisms. Investing in research to create these solutions is crucial for effectively addressing a wide range of AI risks. First, instead of just relying on today’s alignment approaches, it is important to explore new approaches for building safe AI with better safety guarantees. This is a complex challenge, which requires a multifaceted approach. Second, in addition to developing safer models which can require long-term research, it is also important to develop defensive approaches or immune systems in society to reduce the potential negative impacts from misuse of AI technology. For example, additional defensive systems can involve improving the security posture and defenses of computer systems against security risks caused by AI misuse.

Fifth, we need to build trust in the community and unite it instead of fragmentation and polarization. Currently, there is significant polarization within the overall community: While one side is deeply concerned about extreme AI risks and calls for strong measures such as pausing development and strong regulations that will hinder innovation with the hope for better AI safety, the other side claims that we worry about risks too much and that we should focus on innovation without regulating AI at all. This polarization impedes safe AI development. Instead, we need to make a united effort to find the best solutions for fostering innovation while mitigating risks. To achieve this, we recommend having open dialogue and collaborative research initiatives that bring together diverse perspectives. Creating platforms for respectful debate in a trusted setting and shared problem-solving can help bridge the gap between different viewpoints. Additionally, establishing interdisciplinary research groups that include AI researchers, ethicists, policymakers, and industry representatives can promote a more balanced and comprehensive approach to AI development and safety. We hope this approach can also lead to comprehensive international cooperation.

In summary, we believe that we need to develop better understanding of AI risks before deploying highly committal policies.
