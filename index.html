Understanding AI Safety

Home | Full Letter

To address the growing societal impact of AI, many efforts intend to increase safety, including policy initiatives in many jurisdictions. For example, SB-1047, a bill in California proposes to impose liability on model developers for downstream use, to incentivize greater safety investment from model developers. Current proposals for AI regulation have sparked considerable debate in the community, including their potential unintended consequences and hindrance on open-source AI development and innovation.

Increasing AI safety requires a robust scientific foundation and consideration of the potential costs of policy to both covered entities and to society. We argue that there are higher priorities we should explore that may offer more balanced and effective solutions. These alternatives address more immediate and direct needs for improving AI safety and may have fewer negative consequences and less onerous requirements.

To build safer and more reliable AI, we propose that the AI community should first quantitatively understand AI risks and that policymakers should use this understanding to inform policymaking. In particular, if policymakers are to pursue highly committal policy, evidence of the risks of AI should meet a high evidentiary standard. As members of the AI and policy communities with diverse expertise, we agree that it's prudent to first consider and implement the following suggested priorities:

  1. We need to better understand AI risks.

    AI is associated with several poorly understoodrisks. We should invest in research that clarifies the marginal risks of AI, relative to pre-existing technologies like search engines.

  2. We need to increase transparency on AI development.

    AI is a complex and opaque technology: understanding AI relies upon sufficient information. We should develop effective reporting practices for model developers of highly capable models to disclose information about their models.

  3. We need to actively monitor post-deployment AI harms.

    AI can lower the barrier for misuse and increase the volume of attacks, requiring increased monitoring capacity or new techniques for attack detection. We should implement adverse event reporting systems to document and communicate instances of AI harms.

  4. We need to develop and deploy mitigation and defense mechanisms for already identified AI risks.

    Reducing the risks of AI will require new approaches for building AI with better safety guarantees and hardening society to AI misuse. To the extent technical interventions do not suffice, we should consider how technical and policy interventions can jointly increase societal resilience.

  5. We need to build trust and reduce fragmentation in the AI community.

    The overall community is increasingly polarized around the nature of AI risks and the appropriate regulatory responses. We should foster open dialogue and convene different parts of the AI community to identify areas of agreement and build shared understanding of AI risks.
